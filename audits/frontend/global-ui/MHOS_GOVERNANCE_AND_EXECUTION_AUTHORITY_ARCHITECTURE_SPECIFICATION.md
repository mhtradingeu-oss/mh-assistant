# MH-OS Governance and Execution Authority Architecture Specification

**Date:** 2026-05-23  
**Author:** GitHub Copilot

---

## 1. Executive Summary
Governance and execution authority architecture are critical for MH-OS to ensure safe, explainable, and auditable AI-native business operations. This architecture defines the boundaries, visibility, and escalation of all operational power—ensuring that every orchestration, execution, and delegation is governed, explicit, and reviewable. Without constitutional authority models, scalable and trustworthy AI autonomy is impossible.

## 2. Definition of Operational Authority
- **Permission:** Simple allowance to perform an action; not sufficient for operational governance.
- **Access:** Technical ability to reach or use a resource; not operational control.
- **Execution authority:** Canonical right to initiate, perform, or confirm execution.
- **Orchestration authority:** Right to coordinate, delegate, or route operational flows.
- **Escalation authority:** Right to escalate, interrupt, or override operational flows.
- **Operational control:** Constitutional power to govern, approve, or reclaim execution and orchestration.

## 3. Core MH-OS Governance Philosophy
- All authority is explicit, visible, and auditable.
- Execution is always governed and reviewable.
- Delegation and ownership transitions are surfaced and explainable.
- Orchestration is subject to authority boundaries and escalation rules.
- Autonomy is always controlled and bounded by governance.
- All authority transitions are explainable and traceable.

## 4. Global Authority Model
Canonical authority layers:
- **Observation authority** (see, but not act)
- **Recommendation authority** (advise, but not execute)
- **Orchestration authority** (coordinate, delegate, route)
- **Execution authority** (initiate, perform, confirm execution)
- **Escalation authority** (interrupt, escalate, override)
- **Override authority** (emergency or executive reclamation)
- **Autonomous authority** (bounded, governed autonomy)

## 5. AI Execution Authority
- AI may recommend actions and strategies within its domain.
- AI may orchestrate workflows, delegations, and handoffs within governed boundaries.
- AI may execute only within explicit, authorized zones.
- AI must escalate when encountering restricted, high-risk, or ambiguous operations.
- AI may never autonomously perform actions outside its governed authority or in mandatory human confirmation zones.

## 6. Delegation Governance Model
- Delegation rules are explicit and surfaced.
- Ownership transfer requires visible, auditable handoff.
- Escalation delegation is governed by authority inheritance and explicit escalation paths.
- Authority inheritance is always traceable and reviewable.
- Delegation visibility is mandatory for all operational transitions.

## 7. Confirmation & Approval Governance
- Confirmation gates are explicit and surfaced in all workflows.
- Executive approvals are required for high-risk or strategic operations.
- Operational approvals are required for routine but impactful actions.
- AI approval limits are strictly bounded and surfaced.
- Mandatory human confirmation zones are enforced for critical or ambiguous operations.

## 8. Autonomous Execution Boundaries
- Safe autonomy: AI may act autonomously only within governed, low-risk boundaries.
- Unsafe autonomy: AI is strictly prohibited from acting without explicit approval.
- Restricted operations: Require escalation or human confirmation.
- Escalation-required operations: Must be surfaced and routed to higher authority.
- High-risk execution boundaries: Always require explicit, multi-layer approval.

## 9. Executive Override Architecture
- Human override: Human actors may reclaim authority at any time.
- Emergency override: Immediate interruption of execution or orchestration.
- Orchestration interruption: Authority to pause, reroute, or cancel orchestration flows.
- Execution cancellation: Authority to halt or reverse execution.
- Authority reclamation: Executive power to reclaim or redistribute authority.

## 10. Governance Visibility Model
The following must always be visible:
- Who owns execution authority
- Who approved execution
- Who escalated execution
- Why execution occurred
- Current authority holder at every stage

## 11. AI Governance Integrity Rules
What must NEVER happen:
- Silent execution (no visible authority or approval)
- Hidden orchestration (invisible routing or delegation)
- Invisible delegation (no traceable handoff)
- Unauthorized escalation (escalation without authority)
- Authority ambiguity (unclear or disputed authority)
- Uncontrolled autonomy (AI acting outside governed boundaries)

## 12. Future Governance Expansion

- **Autonomous operational systems:** Governed, auditable autonomy.
- **Self-healing orchestration:** Governed, autonomous orchestration recovery.

## 13. Governance-Cognition Relationship
Governance improves:

- Operational safety and risk management

- Scalable, safe AI orchestration

## 14. Operational Authority Maturity Model
Evolution:
- Manual operations → Workflow governance → Orchestration governance → AI operational governance → Autonomous governed infrastructure

## 15. Final Architectural Position
With governance and execution authority architecture complete, MH-OS becomes a constitutionally governed AI-native operating system. All operational power is explicit, bounded, and auditable—enabling safe, scalable, and explainable AI autonomy for business operations.
